Politics of Dashao

Dashao is a large empire with an equally sprawling bureaucracy. There are a bewildering variety of ministerial posts and appointments at every level, from isolated townships to the imperial court. This document outlines the basic strokes.

Imperial Government

The empire as a whole is ruled by the Empress. She is the highest political and military authority, and in theory there is little limit to her power. In practice, however, most of her responsibilities are delegated, and she seldom takes a direct hand in politics.

Beneath the Empress are the three excellencies - the Chancellor, the Grand Commandant, and the Councelor. The Chancellor is largely responsible for the functioning of the civil government, while the Grand Commandant controls the military. The Councelor occupies a more administrative role, overseeing local officials and monitoring abuse of authority.

Each of the three excellencies oversees 3 ministries. In total, these 9 ministries manage all of the affairs of the government at the imperial level.

Provincal Government

The empire is then divided into 10 provinces, with the capital as a special administrative division under the direct control of the court. Each province is controlled by a governor, who is supposed to be centrally appointed. With the waning influence of the court in recent years, however, the governerships have become de-facto hereditary positions.

Each governor additionally oversees provincal auditors and administrators who oversee the smooth functioning of government and commerce in the area.

County Government

Each province contains many counties, which are presided over by magistrates. Magistrates are the lowest level official which is directly appointed by the imperial court. They are responsible for appointing lower level officials, as well as arbiting over legal disputes in their county.

Township Government

Finally, each county is broken up into townships, consisting of towns, cities, and their surrounding lands. Each township is managed by a mayor, who is often chosen by locals, and a chief of police, who is responsible for enforcing the law.

A note on noble titles

While Noble Titles are common, they entail no particular political power. Nobles are merely responsible for collecting taxes from the households on their fief, from which they can draw a (often substantial) income. In practice, the wealth that noble families acquire can make them politically influential, however.
